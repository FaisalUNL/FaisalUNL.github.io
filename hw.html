UIUN Negotiation Reflection

In the workshop, I gained valuable insights into how people perceive and interact with various interfaces. This learning experience highlighted the common needs and preferences users may have.
Our exercise involved providing specific points for different sections such as Color Scheme, Body Heading, Main Page Header, etc. Each company was tasked with identifying a particular need of their target population and prioritizing it based on the urgency and relevance of that need. This initial phase was crucial in establishing a foundation for our subsequent discussions.

Once we listed our points, the next stage involved negotiating with other companies. The aim was to compare and prioritize our listed points against theirs. This negotiation process was dynamic; it involved convincing others of our viewpoint or being convinced by theirs. Interestingly, we also explored the possibility of merging our points with others to create solutions that could cater to a wider range of users.

A significant part of the workshop was dedicated to website design for children under 13. It became clear that this demographic has specific developmental needs. For instance, their evolving literacy skills necessitate the use of simple language and intuitive navigation. They may also face challenges with tasks requiring fine motor skills, underscoring the importance of large, easy-to-click buttons. Based on these insights, my recommendation is to develop websites that are visually appealing yet educational. This can be achieved by using vibrant colors, interactive elements, and straightforward text and voice instructions. The design should also accommodate their motor skills through simple gestures and large touch targets.

Our negotiation sessions were particularly engaging. We discussed points under categories like Dynamically Loaded Content, Main Page Header, and Button/Hyperlinks. These discussions required us to interact with various groups representing different interests, such as Non-Native users and Generation Z. One of the more challenging negotiations was with representatives from rural areas, especially regarding the Dynamically Loaded Content section. Ultimately, we reached a consensus, although we had to settle for Priority 2 instead of our initial goal of Priority 1.

This summit was not just an academic exercise but also a fun and engaging way to learn about perspectives. I realized the importance of being patient, open-minded, and articulate when presenting my ideas. More importantly, I learned to appreciate and critically analyze viewpoints different from my own. These skills are not just valuable in professional settings like summits but are also applicable in everyday conversations.
